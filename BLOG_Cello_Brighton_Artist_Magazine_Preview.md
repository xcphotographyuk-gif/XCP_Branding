## On Playing Outside the Expected

*"I didn't train for years to play in rooms where the sound gets absorbed by corporate carpet,"* she tells me, between takes. *"The city is an instrument. The acoustics of a Victorian arcade, or the natural reverb off a seafront wall — you can't manufacture that."*

Brighton suits her. The city has always had a tolerance — even an appetite — for people doing creative work in unusual places. Street sessions. Private events. Gallery openings. Rooftop gigs. The kind of performances that aren't announced until the day before, if at all.

## Why Document It

There's a question I get from clients before almost every shoot: *why does this need to be photographed?*

The honest answer, for someone like [ARTIST NAME], is that the work is ephemeral. A performance happens once. The specific quality of light on the cello at 4pm in [LOCATION], the way the sound moved through the space, the expression on a passing stranger who stopped without planning to — none of that exists anywhere except in that moment and in the photographs taken during it.

Documentation is not vanity. For a working artist, it is the difference between being discoverable and being invisible.

[ARTIST NAME] needed imagery that could carry her story to people who hadn't heard her play yet. Imagery that communicated the quality, the character, and the environment of what she does — before a single note was heard. That's the brief we worked to.
