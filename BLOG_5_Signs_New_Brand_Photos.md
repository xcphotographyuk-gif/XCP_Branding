## Sign 1: Your Photos Are More Than Two Years Old

This is the most common one, and the most consistently underestimated.

Two years sounds like a long time in photography terms. It isn't. Consider what changes in two years of a growing business:

- Your team grows, or changes
- Your workspace evolves
- Your services expand or sharpen
- Your positioning shifts
- Your own appearance changes

None of this is a problem until your potential clients are comparing the version of you in your photographs to the version of you in a video call, a networking event, or a meeting room. When there's a visible gap, trust erodes. Not dramatically. Not consciously. But it erodes.

The "is that really them?" moment — even a fleeting one — introduces doubt. And doubt in a sales context is expensive.

**The test:** Pull up your website hero image, your LinkedIn profile photo, and your most-used team photograph. Are they still accurate representations of how your business looks and feels today? If you hesitate, you have your answer.

**The exception:** If your business and team are largely unchanged, two-year-old high-quality photographs may still be perfectly serviceable. The metric isn't really age — it's accuracy and quality. But two years is a reasonable prompt to ask the question honestly.

## Sign 2: You're Using Stock Photography Anywhere on Your Core Pages

There's a conversation happening in your potential client's brain when they land on your website that you don't have access to. But we know from research — and from simple human experience — that part of that conversation sounds like this:

*"Is this real?"*

Stock photography fails that question. Not always immediately. Not always consciously. But audiences have become extraordinarily good at spotting the visual language of stock images — the slightly-too-perfect lighting, the demographically-curated groups, the office that looks like every other office, the "candid" conversation that was clearly directed by someone who'd never had a real one.

The moment your audience identifies stock imagery, something shifts. The brand begins to feel like a performance rather than a reality. And that gap between performance and reality is where trust dies.

**Where this matters most:**
- Homepage hero image — this is your single highest-stakes visual
- About page — where people are specifically trying to understand who you actually are
- Services page — where they're deciding whether to trust you with their money
- Social media profiles — where your personal brand lives

**The exception:** Some design-heavy brands use minimal or abstract imagery intentionally as a stylistic choice, not as a substitute for authentic brand photography. There's a difference between a deliberate design decision and a gap-filling workaround.

## Sign 3: You're Reluctant to Send People to Your Website

This one is a bit painful to name, but it's the clearest possible signal.

If you're at a networking event and someone says "great, I'll check out your website" and your internal reaction is anything other than quiet confidence — if there's a small wince, a "oh, it's a bit out of date" disclaimer, a mental note to maybe follow up with some context — your visual presence is not doing its job.

Your website and your social profiles should be your strongest salespeople. They work 24 hours a day, every day, without a lunch break. They speak to every person who considers working with you. They are the first impression for most of your potential clients.

If you're apologising for them, even silently, they're costing you money.

**The honest question:** Would you hand someone your business card with complete confidence that what they find when they look you up reflects the quality, character, and credibility of your business? If not, why not?

## Sign 4: Your Imagery and Your Brand Have Moved Apart

Businesses evolve. Positioning shifts. Services get refined. The story you're telling about your business today may be significantly different from the one you were telling two or three years ago.

The problem is that photography tends to be a one-off investment — done once, then left alone while everything else moves forward. The result is a gradual drift between the visual story your photographs tell and the reality of what your business is today.

**Common examples of this drift:**

- A company that has moved upmarket, but whose photography still reads as mid-market
- A founder who has grown in confidence and clarity, but whose headshots still show the uncertainty of an earlier stage
- A team-based business where the photography still foregrounds solo work
- A business that has moved premises, but whose images still show the old office
- A service business that has dropped certain services, but whose photography still prominently features work from those areas

Any of these create friction. The potential client who encounters imagery that doesn't match the pitch will notice the inconsistency — even if they can't name it. It introduces a background sense that something doesn't add up. And in a service relationship, where the entire purchase is based on trust, "something doesn't add up" is catastrophic.

**The test:** Show a stranger your photography, then show them your most recent pitch document or sales call script. Do they tell the same story? If not, which one is right?

## Sign 5: Your Competitors Look Better Than You Online

I want to be careful here, because "look at your competitors and copy them" is generally terrible advice. You should not be competing on aesthetics with other photographers, other agencies, or other service providers. You should be competing on authenticity — on being the most genuinely represented version of what you actually are.

But if your competitors have visibly invested in professional brand photography and you haven't, there's a practical problem: they are making the decision easier for the client. Better visual presentation creates a stronger first impression. A stronger first impression creates a preference. A preference creates a booking.

This isn't about being prettier. It's about *seeming more trustworthy* — and at the awareness stage of a client relationship, seeming more trustworthy is indistinguishable from being more trustworthy.

**The test:** Go to the LinkedIn profiles of three competitors you respect. Go to their websites. Look at their imagery with fresh eyes. How does it compare to yours? Not in terms of who looks more "designed," but in terms of who feels more real, more credible, more worth a conversation?

If the answer isn't you, you know what to do.

## The Cumulative Effect

The insidious thing about these five signs is that each one individually might feel manageable. Photos are a bit old — fine. One stock image on the about page — people don't look that closely. Website feels a bit dated — the work speaks for itself, right?

Individually, each of these things creates a small amount of friction in the client's decision-making process. A small hesitation. A small doubt. But friction is cumulative. And in a market where clients have multiple options and limited time, cumulative friction is the difference between an enquiry and a scroll-past.

If you're nodding at more than one of these five signs, the friction in your visual presence is likely costing you real revenue. Not dramatically — it's not a tap that's been turned off. It's a slow drip. But over months and years, slow drips add up.

---

## What to Do If You Recognise These Signs

**Step 1: Audit honestly.**  
Go through your website, LinkedIn, and most-used social profiles with a deliberately critical eye. Note specifically what's out of date, inaccurate, or unconvincing. Write it down.

**Step 2: Identify the biggest gaps.**  
Not everything needs to change at once. If your homepage hero is strong but your About page uses a three-year-old headshot, start there. Prioritise the highest-traffic, highest-stakes pages.

**Step 3: Have a conversation before you commit.**  
Brand photography is a significant investment and the brief matters enormously. Before booking, have a proper conversation with a photographer about your business, your goals, and how the imagery will be used. A photographer who doesn't ask these questions won't produce photography that works hard for your business.

**Step 4: Think library, not one-offs.**  
The goal of a brand photography session should be a content library — 20, 50, 100 images that serve your business across every platform and touchpoint for the next 2-3 years. Think about where you'll use the images before you shoot them, not after.
